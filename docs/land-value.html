Research Track 03

Land Value & Game Board

Does the Shanghai Millionaire game board encode colonial spatial hierarchy rather than empirical land values?

Core Hypothesis

Research Question

The Shanghai Millionaire game board reflects colonial spatial hierarchy rather than empirical market land values. Streets in "Chinese commercial areas" may be systematically undervalued compared to their actual rental rates, revealing the "colonial gaze" embedded in the game's design.

The Board: Price Structure

Shanghai Millionaire follows the British Waddington's Monopoly template: 40 squares, 8 color groups, prices increasing clockwise from GO. The localization replaces London streets with Shanghai streets while maintaining the price structure.

The game uses the British color scheme: Dark Blue ($350-400) as the most expensive, Purple ($60-80) as the cheapest.

Research Questions

Q1: Do game prices correlate with empirical land values? Ongoing

Collect SMC Annual Report property assessments and newspaper rental advertisements to compare with game prices. Focus on the pink group anomaly: were Ningpo/Tientsin/Honan actually lower-value, or are they undervalued in the colonial perspective?

Q2: How did the 1937-1941 "Solitary Island" period affect land values? Planned

After the 1937 Battle of Shanghai, the International Settlement became an isolated "solitary island" surrounded by Japanese occupation. Refugee influx caused dramatic shifts in land values. Does the game (likely printed 1938-1940) reflect pre-war colonial imagination or wartime reality?

Q3: What does comparison with other Monopoly versions reveal? Planned

Atlantic City (1935) and London (1936) versions have been studied for their price-value correlations. How does Shanghai's colonial context make its localization unique? Is Shanghai the only major version where localization involves colonial power asymmetry?

Q4: Who determined the value hierarchy? Planned

The game's value system reflects whose perspective? British colonial administrators? International Settlement business elites? Or is it a pragmatic adaptation of the London board's structure without local knowledge?

Historical Context

1935

Charles Darrow's Monopoly published by Parker Brothers (US). Based on Atlantic City, with prices loosely reflecting real estate values.

1936

Waddington's publishes British version with London streets. Mayfair and Park Lane correspond to actual high-value areas.

1937

Battle of Shanghai (August-November). Chinese city falls to Japanese forces. International Settlement and French Concession remain unoccupied "solitary island."

1938-1940

Massive refugee influx into the Settlement. Land values in previously "Chinese" areas appreciate dramatically. Shanghai Millionaire likely printed during this period.

1941

Pacific War begins. Japanese occupy the International Settlement (December). Game production likely ceases.

1946

Lobel brothers create handmade Shanghai Millionaire in refugee camp. Their choice of this game suggests it was well-known in the international community.

Data Sources Needed

SMC Annual Reports

Shanghai Municipal Council annual reports (1935-1941) contain property tax assessments and land value indices. Available at Shanghai Municipal Archives and some university libraries.

Newspaper Archives

North China Daily News, The China Press, and Shanghai Times contain real estate advertisements and rental market reports. Available via ProQuest Historical Newspapers.

Academic Datasets

Virtual Shanghai (virtualshanghai.net) and Peking University Historical GIS Center have Shanghai land use data from the 1930s.

Call for collaboration: If you have access to any of these sources or relevant expertise, please contact us.

Related Research

Atlantic City Studies

Ralph Anspach's research (documented in The Billion Dollar Monopoly Swindle) established that Atlantic City prices were loosely based on real values but adjusted for gameplay. This provides methodology for our Shanghai study.

Shanghai Colonial Space

Christian Henriot's Shanghai, 1927-1937 and Wen-hsin Yeh's Shanghai Splendor provide theoretical frameworks for understanding colonial spatial hierarchy and consumption culture.
