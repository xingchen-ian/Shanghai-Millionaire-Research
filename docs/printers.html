Printer Tracing

Key Inference: Producing this game required color printing capability (board + currency + deed cards), cardboard box manufacturing, English typesetting, and access to the expatriate retail market.

Candidate Ranking

#1

Millington Limited 英商美灵登有限公司

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
TypeBritish-owned advertising & printing company
Founded1927 by Francis C. Millington
LocationNanking Road → Hong Kong Road (Central Garage building)
CapabilitiesCommercial color printing, advertising, publishing
ScaleIssued stock in 1937; HSBC was a major shareholder (1,000 shares × $4)

Why top candidate: A British advertising and printing firm serving the expatriate market — exactly the kind of business that would take on a commercial print run of an English-language board game. The company also ran the "Millington Art School," indicating creative production capacity.

Next steps: Search Millington's 1937–1940 commercial printing order records; look for Millington ads in the North-China Daily News.

#2

Cuihua Card Factory 上海粹华卡片厂

⭐⭐⭐⭐
TypeChinese-owned playing card manufacturer
Founded1919 by Huang Disheng
LocationZhengjiamuqiao, Shanghai (now Fujian Middle Road)
Key milestone1931: Imported German equipment, began commercial playing card production
Ceased1937 (Japanese occupation of Shanghai)

Why strong candidate: China's first playing card factory — its product line (cards, certificates, invitations) is a direct match for board game components. The "Circus Clown" (马戏丑角) brand proved its color card-printing capability. However, 1937 shutdown is problematic if the game was produced after 1937.

Next steps: Check if Cuihua accepted English-language game commissions; examine product catalogs from 1931–1937.

#3

Kelly & Walsh Limited 别发洋行 / 别发印书局

⭐⭐⭐⭐
TypeBritish-owned publisher, printer, bookseller
Founded1876 (merger of Kelly & Co. and F. & C. Walsh)
LocationThe Bund No. 11 → Nanking Road (from 1918)
CapabilitiesHigh-quality printing "of every description," lithography, stationery
ProductsEnglish books, postcards, souvenir albums (e.g., Shanghai of To-Day)
Post-1941Relocated to Hong Kong after Japanese occupation

Why plausible: Shanghai's premier English-language publisher with proven color printing quality and expatriate market access. No evidence of game products, but their "every description" printing claim leaves the door open.

Second Tier

#4

North-China Daily News Press 字林西报馆印刷部

⭐⭐⭐

Shanghai's largest English newspaper, with industrial printing facilities that sometimes took commercial print jobs. Ceased publication 1941–45 under Japanese occupation — matches the production window of ~1938–1940.

#5

The Commercial Press 商务印书馆

⭐⭐⭐

Shanghai's largest printing enterprise, with full color printing and English typesetting (20-year partnership with Macmillan). But its business model centered on Chinese-language textbooks, and wartime damage (1932 bombing, 1937 battle) severely constrained capacity.

Third Tier

Chung Hwa Book Co. 中华书局 ⭐⭐ — Similar to Commercial Press but less English capability.
Ikeda Printing Co. 池田印刷株式会社 ⭐⭐⭐ — Japanese-owned color printing; unlikely to print British pirated games after 1937.
Russian Emigre Presses ⭐⭐ — Small-scale, primarily Russian-language; insufficient commercial capacity.
Jewish Refugee Presses ⭐⭐ — Small German/Yiddish newspapers; no evidence of color printing capability.

Time Window Analysis

Before 1937

All candidates possible. Cuihua Card Factory is the strongest match — it was actively producing playing cards and had German printing equipment.

1937–1941

Cuihua shut down. Japanese firms unlikely. Millington and Kelly & Walsh rise to the top — British firms still operating in the Settlement, serving the expatriate market.

1941–1945

NCDN closed. Kelly & Walsh fled to Hong Kong. Only Japanese or neutral-capital presses remained operational — making commercial production of an English game highly unlikely.
