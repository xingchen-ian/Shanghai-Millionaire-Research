Camps & Refugees

Internment camps, the Hongkew Ghetto, and games as wartime survival.

Japanese Internment Camps (1943–1945)

After Pearl Harbor (December 1941), Japan occupied Shanghai's International Settlement. In January 1943, the Japanese military government began concentrating Allied civilians — British, American, Dutch, Australian, and others — into designated camps across Shanghai. Approximately 12,000 people were interned in at least 20 facilities.

Games & Culture in the Camps

Despite brutal conditions, internees organized remarkably rich cultural lives — a survival mechanism documented across multiple camps:

  • Lunghwa Academy — Herbert Huckstep and others established a school for ~350 children, using brown paper bags as writing materials
  • Jazz bands — Professional musicians from Shanghai's dance halls (Little Club, Metropole Ballroom) formed ensembles, playing standards like There'll Be Some Changes Made and Nagasaki
  • Bridge tournaments, lectures, theatrical performances — Betty Barr (last surviving Shanghai-based Lunghua internee) recalled earning her Girl Guide camping badge as "the happiest yet most ironic memory"
  • Secret radios — Two hidden radios in Lunghua camp allowed internees to follow war news

This cultural resilience provides the context for understanding why the Lobel brothers would spend effort making a board game in 1946 — even after liberation, the habit of creating entertainment from nothing persisted.

The Hongkew Ghetto

In parallel with the Allied internment camps, approximately 20,000 Jewish refugees from Germany, Austria, and Poland lived in Shanghai — most in the Hongkew (虹口) district. After February 1943, Japan designated a "Restricted Area for Stateless Refugees" (无国籍难民指定区域), effectively creating a ghetto.

~1 sq mi Designated area
14,245 Jewish refugees confined
~100,000 Chinese residents in same area

Life in the Hongkew Ghetto was constrained but not as tightly controlled as the internment camps. Refugees could sometimes leave for work (with passes), and a rich community life developed: the Kadoorie School educated 500+ children, three German-language newspapers were published, and cultural events flourished.

The Lobel family — Siegfried and Manfred's parents — fled Berlin in September 1940 and were forced into the Hongkew designated area in 1942. The boys' 1946 hand-drawn Shanghai Millionaire was made after liberation, using surplus US Army K-ration boxes — a material artifact of their displaced lives.

Could Shanghai Millionaire Have Entered the Camps?

Against

  • Japanese authorities confiscated personal belongings upon internment — non-essentials were seized
  • Internees prioritized food, clothing, medicine over games
  • Overcrowded living quarters left no space for large board games
  • Commercial production ceased by ~1940; the game was already scarce by 1943
  • No direct documentary or physical evidence found

For

  • Strong entertainment need in camps — internees organized bridge, theater, music
  • ~350 children in Lunghwa alone needed activities
  • The handmade Lobel copy proves the desire for games existed in the refugee community
  • Board size (48.5cm²) is relatively portable
Assessment: Commercial Shanghai Millionaire entering the internment camps is unlikely (20–30%). However, the possibility of internees improvising similar games from memory is moderate (40–50%), consistent with documented cultural activities.
